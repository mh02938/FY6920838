Introduction 

        The F35 Lightning II is the most advanced multi role combat aircraft to ever exist, to 
        obtain this title engineers at Lockheed Martin combined an incredible weapon system 
        with unparalleled stealth technologies. These have allowed the F35 Lightning II to 
        dominate the skies and remain almost undetectable since it first entered service in 
        2015. This paper will focus on the specifically on the aircraft’s stealth systems, 
        which allow it to enter enemy airspace while avoiding detection by enemy radar and 
        other aircraft.

Radar Absorbing Materials 

        The first of the main radar countermeasures aboard the F35 is the material that coats its 
        outer layer. Whilst the specific material used to coat the fuselage of the F35 has not 
        been publicly disclosed, we do know that it is a type of radar absorbent material (RAM). 
        To understand how RAMs work we must first understand how radar works: a simple radar 
        consists of a electromagnetic (EM) wave transmitter and receiver, it sends out a wave at 
        the speed of light and waits for it to return, if the wave returns to the receiver then 
        the radar know it was reflected by another object and uses the speed of the radio wave and 
        the time it took from when the wave was transmitted to when it was received to calculate 
        the how far away the object is (Lockheed Martin, 2021). RAM coatings absorbs a high 
        proportion of these waves and converts them into heat energy which is dissipated throughout 
        the airframe of the aircraft, meaning that very little EM waves are reflected back to the 
        radar receiver, this results in the aircraft having a very small radar cross-section or RCS 
        (the perceived size of an object through radar) (Mouritz, 2012). The RAM coatings used on 
        the F35 are baked into the aircraft’s skin panels meaning that even during deployments on 
        aircraft carriers, where the coating can become discoloured and oxidise, there is a 
        negligible effect on stealth performance (Newdick, 2022). In fact the coating is claimed to 
        get better with each flight as the coating becomes smoother, causing less scattering of em 
        waves (Axe, 2012).
